\begin{frame}{Introduction \& Aims}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item In this short tutorial I want to give an overview of how correspondence analysis can be helpful for understanding categorical/compositional data.
    \item I am skipping all of the maths and as much of the theory as possible in order to focus on some intuition for the CA results, as well as to get through some informative examples.
    \item If you want to know more, I strongly recommend the textbook \textit{Correspondence Analysis in Practice, Third Edition} by Michael Greenacre\nocite{greenacre2017}.
    \item All the code for the examples, including the source for these slides, is in a public GitHub repository, here: \url{https://github.com/whatf0xx/ca4cm}. It is mostly based off of the Python package \href{https://github.com/MaxHalford/prince}{\texttt{prince}}\nocite{Halford_Prince}.
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{\enquote{Correspondence analysis is PCA for categorical data}}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item In a large, multi-variate data set, we often use PCA to identify which parts of the data provide the best low-dimensional description of the data.
    \item This relies on the data being \textit{ordered}, which is trivially true for nominal data, and a reasonable reach for binary data - but what about categorical data?
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Visualizing categorical data}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item It doesn't make sense to think of an ordering with categorical data for $n > 2$.
    \item Imagine we had a survey with multiple, distinct outcomes for participants to select. How could we visualize the results of such a survey?
  \end{itemize}
  \uncover<2>{
    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[height=0.48\textheight]{abc_mobility.png}
      \caption{Barycentric coordinates for mobility in cities around the world, from our very own \textcite{prieto-curielABCMobility2024}.}
    \end{figure}
  }
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Extending this to higher dimensions}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The key point that lets up represent the mobility data on a traingle like this is that the category counts (proportions) have to sum up to 1.
    \item Effectively, this is transforming our \textit{categorical data} into textit{compositional data}.
    \item As a result, we lose a \textbf{degree of freedom} compared to representing these as distances along orthogonal axes, and consequently we can display $N$ categories in only $N-1$ dimensions. 
  \end{itemize}
  \begin{figure}
    \centering

    \caption{Simplices in different dimensions. Illustration from \parencite{chungPersistentHomologyApproach2019}.}
  \end{figure}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Key features of correspondence analysis}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The correspondence analysis (CA) gives the representation of the data \textit{rows} within the simplex formed by the \textit{column} points.
        \item From this, just like with PCA, we can then find the most descriptive dimensions within the data.
        \item However, the CA also does some extra work for us, by deforming the geometry of the space where the data lives.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Correspondence analysis geometry 1}
    Returning to the hair/eye color example, consider the origin point: in CA, this represents simultaneously the \textit{average row profile} \textbf{and} the \textit{average column profile}.
    \begin{figure}
        \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.4\textwidth}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../2d-color.pdf}
        \end{subfigure}
        \hfill
        \begin{subfigure}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
            \centering
            \vspace{-3.5cm}
            \footnotesize
            \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
              \toprule
              & \textbf{Brown} & \textbf{Blue} & \textbf{Hazel} & \textbf{Green} \\
              \midrule
              \textbf{Black} & 68 & 20 & 15 & 5 \\
              \textbf{Brown} & 119 & 84 & 54 & 29 \\
              \textbf{Red}   & 26 & 17 & 14 & 14 \\
              \textbf{Blond} & 7 & 94 & 10 & 16 \\
              \bottomrule
            \end{tabular}
        \end{subfigure}
    \end{figure}
    So \textit{row points} (i.e. hair colors) close to the origin have a close-to-average \textit{column profile}: knowing this hair color tells you little more than the average for predicting eye color.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Symmetric biplots}
    Instead of plotting the columns as the vertices of a simplex and the rows as points within it, we can treat both as though they are on the same \enquote{scale}.
    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.58\textwidth]{../symmetric_wahlkreis.pdf}
        \caption{Symmetric biplot of the voting data. Similar rows (\textit{Bezirke}) are close together, as are similar columns (parties), but row-column distances are less meaningful than before.}
    \end{figure}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{CA recap}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item When we have \textit{compositional} data (i.e. counts of categorical data) in a contingency table, we can represent row profiles as points within a simplex formed of the columns of the table.
        \item CA does some extra work for us at this step, by centering the origin to the \textbf{average profile}.
        \item This also means distance from the origin reflects deviation from independence: points far out are informative about structure.
        \item Points that are close together in the CA are statistically equivalent (i.e. Mariahilf and Neubau have similar voting patterns; being brown vs. black haired doesn't help you guess eye color).
        \item CA results are displayed on asymmetric or symmetric biplots depending on the data and what the author wants to communicate.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
